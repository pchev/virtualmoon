CONFIGURE THE VIRTUAL MOON ATLAS

A good configuration of the "Virtual Moon Atlas" is necessary to enjoy the most of its possibilities.


Once the program has started, click on the "Configuration" menu. Click on the "General" tab. Enter the coordinates of your observation site, which will include the calculation of daytime libration. Preferably choose the "Topocentric" option for the most faithful representation possible.

Preferably also choose to use date & time from the computer by checking the box to stay in real time while using the "Virtual Moon Atlas".

Then choose the language to be used. English and French delivered with the basic version concerns the program and the database. In other languages ​​produced by other volunteer colleagues, there is usually the program interface only. Some databases have also been translated too, but only in certain languages.

If you have installed only the basic package and wish to obtain other components, connect to the Internet and click on the "Check for optional features" button to see a list of what you can download.


Click on the "Database" tab to choose the databases whose formations names will be displayed on the map, simply by checking the desired databases.

Please note: Displaying the "Unnamed" databases will display up to more than 1 million formations and may slow down operations and display. The "User's database from DATLUN " frame shows the name of your own databases that you will import later using the DATLUN (c) module.


Then click on the "Display" tab. You can also apply the color you want for the point marking the selected formation (Mark), its name (Identification) and the names of the other formations displayed (Label). You can also decide to display them by checking or unchecking the "Display labels" and "Display marks" boxes.

By checking the box "Mark the point of maximum libration", a clearly visible arrow will appear around the lunar disc to indicate you where the libration will be maximum for the date and time that you will then choose. You will notice with use that the size of this arrow is proportional to the intensity of the libration.

The "Mark size" slider allows you to enlarge or reduce the size of the point showing the selected formation.

The "Show label" check box is used to display formation names. You can choose to have the name of a formation covering this formation by checking "Center label on the formation". Otherwise the name will be displayed on the right of the formation.

By checking the "Short label" box, the names of the "Satellites" or "Unnamed" formations will be reduced to a letter or a point so as not to clutter up the display according to their big number.

The slider "Label density" allows you to increase or reduce the number of formation names displayed on the map at the same time.


Then click on the "Texture" tab. First you need to decide what texture rendering you wish. For a view as close as possible to what you will see in the eyepiece, check the "Phase with dynamic relief" box which will introduce shadows into the craters at the terminator. In this case, you will not be able to choose any other texture to apply.

If you want to choose a specific texture to apply, you must check the "Phase without relief" box. Then choose the texture you want to see displayed according to the zoom level by checking the boxes in the matrix. You will see that some textures only go up to level 8 while others go up to 200. We refer you to the VMA manual for the descriptions and the advantages of each texture.

Finally, if you simply want to study certain physical quantities (Concentration in elements, gravimetry, neutron fluxes, etc...), it may be interesting not to apply a texture by checking the "No texture" box.


Now you have to click on the "Overlay" tab if you want to use the scientific layers. To activate them, check "Show overlay". Then choose the layer from the "Overlay" drop-down list. Adjust the transparency of the layer using the "Transparency" slider.

You can also in this tab choose to display superimposed grids of coordinates on the lunar globe by checking the box "Show grid" and moving the cursor to vary the density of the grid.


Now click on the "Images" tab. You will find the list of available image directories. If you have installed only the basic package and wish to obtain other image libraries, connect to the Internet and click on the "Check for optional features" button to see the list of what appears possible to download.


Also click on the "Eyepieces" tab. Enter the list of eyepieces you have. Fill in the "Field in minutes" column using the "Compute" button above. We advise you to classify your eyepieces in ascending order of magnification. By clicking one or more times in the right columns, you can activate (Green) or deactivate (Red) the right / left inversion (<->) or up / down inversion (N / S) functions. So if you own, for example, a Newtonian telescope and a refractor, you can pre-program the exact views that each of these instruments will give you.


If you practice lunar imagery, click on the "CCD" tab. Enter the list of cameras you own. Fill in the "Width" and "Height" columns, using the "Compute" button above. This implies that you know the size and the number of pixels of your sensor. The dimension of the image field is given in minutes of arc.


Then click on the "Printing" tab. Start by defining the margin of the documents to be printed. We recommend 10 mm to be able to show everything on the same page. The "Information text width" slider allows you to choose the size of the characters used to print the information in the database. We advise you to experiment to find the correct setting for your printer.

Check the boxes for the documents you want to print next. The three documents can be printed on the same page if you have chosen a sufficiently small font size. Finally, we advise you to check the "White background" box to limit the use of ink when printing cards.

Finally, confirm your configuration choices by clicking on the "OK" button. For some choices, you will be forced to quit the program and restart it.


Once the program has been launched after configuration, it appears with the map. Observe its behavior when you zoom in and move it. Is it erratic, jerky ? If yes, the settings need to be refined.

Then click on the "Setup" tab on the right. This includes an interesting tool for the final configuration. At the bottom of the frame, you see a "Refresh ... fps" frames per second counter. The higher the number, the smoother the display.

Drag the "Penumbra" slider and choose the degree of transparency that you like best. But be aware that the display is a little faster when the dark part is completely black.

Slide the "Diffuse" tab to the left. It manages the general quality of the map rendering.

Slide the "Specular" tab to the left. It manages the width of the lighting blur at the terminator. The display gains speed if it is positioned to the left.

But the setting that most influences the fluidity of the display is the one set by the "Smoothness" slider. This cursor is used to manage the precision of the sphere on which the surface texture is applied. Placed completely to the left, it is no longer even a sphere, but a faceted polyhedron.

By moving the cursor to the right, you increase the number of these facets, but this setting also becomes increasingly greedy in material resources. However, it is better to go towards a higher spherical resolution because this influences the accuracy of the pointing of the formations on the map.

You now need to play on these different sliders to obtain an acceptable compromise between definition and fluidity. It is not recommended to go below a display rate of 4 fps as this may block the computer.

It is better not to apply "Texture compression". You can also check the "Anti-Alias" box to improve the quality of the map, but this will have an impact on the fluidity of the display.

To finish, click on the "Tools" tab on the right. Check the "South" box in the "Default orientation" frame if you are using a Newton telescope so that the display is oriented like the view in this type of telescope. Or otherwise just tick the "Mirror" box if you are using a refractor, a Maksutov telescope or a Schmidt - Cassegrain telescope. If neither of these two boxes is checked, the Moon will be represented as it is seen with the naked eye or with binoculars.

If you use an instrument on an azimutal mount or a Dobsonian telescope, check the "Local zenith on top" box.

Once these settings have been made, you can use the "Virtual Moon Atlas" effectively. Of course, you can modify them at any time to suit your specific needs.



SEE THE MOON AT A SPECIFIC TIME AND DATE TO PREPARE AN OBSERVATION

You want to know the appearance of the Moon for the next days in order to prepare an observation. This can be for two reasons:
- You wish to observe the "Deep Sky" and the presence of the Moon is rather undesirable.
- You want to observe the Moon and know the formations that will be on the terminator.

When launching the program, go to the "Configuration" menu and check the "Phase" and "Libration" boxes or press the corresponding buttons in the button bar.

Once the program is launched and displayed, click on the "Ephemeris" tab.
- If it is an immediate observation, click on the "Now" button and the program will synchronize with the "System" date of your operating system.
- If it is a future observation, set the date and time with the upper boxes and click on the "Compute" button.

The lunar globe now shows the phase and libration for the date and time you have chosen.

By clicking on the "Ephemeris" tab, you will have access to all the necessary datas. Then click on the "Terminator" tab. In the lower right window, you have access to the list of formations located on the terminator only.

You can limit this list to formations according to their observation interest by choosing the desired level of interest in the upper drop-down list. You can also reduce this list to the formations easily observable in your instrument by choosing the one whose diameter is closest to yours in the drop-down list "Instrument".

You can then classify the generated list according to the name, the interest, the latitude of the formation by checking the corresponding box.

Now click on one of the formations in the list and you will see its position displayed on the lunar disc. By going down or up in the list with the arrow keys on your keyboard, you will be able to locate the position of the other formations and prepare your observation session if this is the case.

At any time, you can see the data relating to the chosen formation by clicking on the "Information" tab on the right.
RECOGNIZE FORMATIONS IN EYEPIECE WHEN ON THE FIELD

You want to know the appearance of a region of the Moon in your telescope in order to recognize the formations immediately in the field of view or to prepare an observation. This research implies that you have previously entered the characteristics of your eyepieces and your instruments in the "Eyepieces" tab of the "Configuration" menu as indicated in the above paragraph "Configure the "Virtual Moon Atlas".

When launching the program, go to the "Configuration" menu and check the "Phase" and "Libration" boxes or press the corresponding buttons in the button bar.

Then go to the "Texture" tab of the "Configuration" menu and check the box at the top "Phase with dynamic relief".

Then go to the "Tools" tab on the right and check only "South" if you use a Newton type telescope or check "North" and "Mirror" if it is a refractor or a catadioptric telescope with a diagonal mirror. You can also click on the appropriate buttons in the button bar. If it is an instrument on an azimuthal mount or on a Dobson mount, also check "Local zenith on top".

Then click on the "Ephemeris" tab on the right.
- If it is an immediate observation, click on the "Now" button and the program will synchronize with the "System" date of your operating system.
- If it is a future observation, set the date and time with the upper boxes and click on the "Compute" button.

The lunar globe now shows the phase and libration and orientation of the lunar globe for the date, time and type of instrument you have chosen. Right-click to bring up the context menu. Click on the "Eyepieces" line and choose the eyepiece / instrument combination used.

A circular field is placed in the "Map" window. You can enlarge its diameter with the zoom slider in the button bar. You can move it by left-clicking inside, holding the button down and moving the mouse. Place the region you are actually observing or planning to observe in the circle.

Then go to the "Database" tab of the "Configuration" menu to choose the necessary databases. The "Named" base is the basic one for general observation through the eyepiece. You can add the "Satellites" base with the "letterd" formations in the "Alphonsus A" style. If you want specific observations, you can also choose the "Domes", "Pyroclastic deposits", "Historic sites", etc. databases.

You then have a good basis of comparison to be able to recognize the formations in the field of the eyepiece. Now click on one of the formations on the map and you can see the data relating to this formation by clicking on the "Information" tab on the right.



FIND AN UNKNOWN LUNAR FORMATION YOU JUST LEARNED ABOUT

You have just discovered in a review or a work dealing with the Moon, the existence of an interesting lunar formation which you did not know existed. The "Virtual Moon Atlas" will indicate its position.

Start the program. For our example, we will choose "Mons Rümker" that your new source of information has just revealed to you. Click on the "Information" tab on the right. At the bottom of the tab, in the "Find" area, type in capital letters, without accent or umlaut:

MONS RUMKER

Then click on the "Find" button. In the following seconds, you see the position of Mons Rümker to the northwest of the lunar disc and the "Information" tab displays the data relating to this formation.

Note that this search function is very powerful because you can use "* wildcards". So if you typed:

MONS *

you can see the position of all the formations whose official name contains "MONS". Just click on the "Next" button to display them one by one.




FIND AN UNKNOWN LUNAR FORMATION WITH A GOTO MOUNT

You have just discovered in a review or a work dealing with the Moon, the existence of an interesting lunar formation which you did not know existed. If you have a computerized "Goto" mount which accepts the ASCOM protocol in particular, the "Virtual Moon Atlas" will position you directly near this formation on the lunar disc.

Click on the "Tools" tab on the right. In the "Telescope" frame, at the bottom, choose from the drop-down list the mount control protocol you want to use. ASCOM is recommended because it allows simultaneous access by the "Virtual Moon Atlas" and by planetarium programs (as long as these do not give contradictory orders). Be sure to install the latest version of the ASCOM drivers from the site:

http://ascom-standards.org/

You have to start by initializing the telescope as usual. Connect your mount to the computer. Start your computer and launch the "Virtual Moon Atlas". Then use the telescope commands or a planetarium program to point to the Moon.

In the "Tools" tab on the right, click on the "Show menu" button and launch the connection.

Start by centering a well-recognizable formation in the eyepiece, and select it on the map. Press "Sync selected" to initialize the coordinates of the telescope at this position. It is also possible to do this operation on a star close to the Moon with the planetarium program.

Then check "Track position" so that the map always displays the position of the telescope.

If your telescope is capable of automatic pointing you can now click on a formation on the map, or choose it with the "Find" input area of ​​the "Information" tab, then click on the "Goto selected" button. If your telescope has been precisely stationed, the selected formation should be near the center of a low magnification eyepiece.



STUDY A KNOWN FORMATION AND ITS OBSERVATION POSSIBILITIES

You want to observe a formation which interests you particularly or you have just discovered in a review or a work dealing with the Moon, the interest of this lunar formation. The "Virtual Moon Atlas" will tell you all the information it contains on this subject and at what period you will be able to observe it at best.

Start the program. We will choose for our example "Alphonsus", a well-known crater that your new source of information has just revealed to you as having a particular interest, for example, the 3 black spots at the bottom of the arena which are probably deposits of volcanic ashes (Pyroclastic deposits).

As you know Alphonsus well, you push the "Zoom" slider in the middle. You can also use your mouse wheel. The map of the Moon is growing.

You click on it with the left button and, while remaining pressed, you move the mouse to simultaneously move the map in order to center Alphonsus. You can also enter "Alphonsus" in the "Find" area at the bottom of the "Information" tab and click on the "Center fromation" button in the button bar.

Alphonsus is now visible in the center of the map. Click on its center. Its name is displayed on the map in the color you have chosen for "Identification" in the "Display" tab of the "Configuration" menu, confirming that you have not mistaken the crater.

As soon as Alphonsus is identified, the map shows you that it is a rather young crater with a central peak and substantial walls.

You can read more detailed information about it in the "Information" tab on the right. You will find there information on the origin of its name, its type, its geology, its position, its dimensions, the pages of the main lunar atlases concerning it, a complete description, advice for observation, the official data of the International Astronomical Union.

Also note the "Profile" frame above the window which gives you an idea of ​​the section of this crater when its depth is well known.

To better understand the description, click on the "Image" button in the button bar. This will launch the PHOTLUN 'c) module. A strip of vignettes showing the photographs of this formation available in the "Virtual Moon Atlas" photo library is displayed. These photos show you a luxury of details sometimes going beyond what is visible in the best terrestrial instruments. The number of images depends on the formation, but also on the image libraries that you have integrated into the ATLUN (c) module from the "Images" tab of the "Configuration" menu.

But you also sometimes have several images taken from different angles and reported by the Apollo missions. There are also excerpts from high resolution lunar maps. Click on the photo you choose to display and it will appear in a separate window.

You can enlarge or reduce this image by clicking on the "Zoom +" and "Zoom -" buttons it contains. The "Elevators" allow you to scroll it if it is too large. Other buttons are used to adjust the brightness and contrast of the chosen image. For more details, we refer you to the specific model of the PHOTLUN  (c) module.

The photos clearly show the black spots, the central peak and the grooves which are indicated in the description of the crater and even more.

You also can learn more about these pyroclastic deposits if you use the "Pyroclastic deposits" database in the "Database" tab of the "Configuration menu.


LOOK FOR A LIBRATION ZONE FORMATION AND ITS NEXT OBSERVATION DATE

Let us now discover an important facet of the power of the "Virtual Moon Atlas".

It is a question of looking for a formation located in the libration zone and which is therefore only periodically visible favorably. For this example, we will use the mythical "MARE ORIENTALE" ... which is no longer in the East, but now in the West since the "International Astronomical Union" definitively fixed the cardinal lunar points.

Start the program. If necessary, come back in the "Visible Face" mode by clicking back on the "Whole globe"  button in the button bar.

Click on the "Information" tab. In the "Find" input box at the bottom, type in capital letters, without accent or umlaut :

EASTERN MARE

Then click on the "Find" button. In the following seconds, you will see the position of Mare Orientale in the Southwest of the lunar globe, perhaps beyond the edge.

You drag the cursor "Zoom" in the middle. The globe of the Moon is getting bigger.

You click on it with the left button and, while remaining pressed, you move the mouse to simultaneously move the globe in order to center Mare Orientale or you click on the "Center formation" button in the button bar.

You can read more detailed information about it in the "Information" tab. You will find there information on the origin of its name, on its position, its dimensions, a complete description and advice on observation, etc...

Click on the "Configuration" menu, then the "Display" tab and activate the "Phase" and "Libration" check boxes or click on the appropriate buttons in the button bar.

Click on the "Ephemeris" tab and use the ">>" button to advance in time at the rate of 1 day per click.

You will then see the slow swing of the lunar globe in accelerated, as well as the succession of the passage of the terminators. Note also the arrow which indicates to you around the periphery of the lunar globe where the libration is maximum and how its size varies according to the intensity of the libration.

Observe how the Mare Orientale becomes more widely visible from time to time. A good observation period takes place with a favorable liberation and a Full Moon day. After the sufficient number of clicks, the date of the boxes in the ephemeris window indicates the next good period. These are rare and it remains to be hoped that thispecific evening, you will be free and that the weather will be good !

To get an idea of ​​the real aspect of the formations of the libration zone which are very distorted when seen from the Earth, click on the "Image" button in the button bar. This will launch the PHOTLUN (c) module. A strip of thumbnails showing the available photographs of the selected formation in the "Virtual Moon Atlas" photo library is displayed. These photos show you a luxury of details beyond what is visible in the best terrestrial instruments. The number of images depends on the formation, but also on the image libraries that you have integrated into the "Virtual Moon Atlas" from the "Images" tab of the "Configuration" menu.

You can enlarge or reduce this image by clicking on the "Zoom +" and "Zoom -" buttons it contains. The elevators allow you to scroll it if it is too large. Other buttons are used to adjust the brightness and contrast of the chosen image.

You can also click on the "Full globe" button in the buttons bar to be able to turn the lunar globe and observe the formation from above by clicking on the "Center" button in the buttons bar.




SEE A FORMATION IN ITS SCIENTIFIC CONTEXT

You can also, thanks to the "Virtual Atlas of the Moon", obtain information on the composition, the gravimetry, the geological nature of the formations and on the terrains which surround them.

Start the program. Go to the "Configuration" menu. Select the "Overlay" tab. Search for the desired layer in the drop-down list and select there. Put a check mark in the "Show layer" box. Click on the "OK" button. The scientific layer is superimposed on the basic texture. To make the layer more visible, you can remove the texture by checking the "No texture" box at the top of the "Texture" tab of the "Configuration" menu. However, we advise you to use the textures "Airbrush without albedo" or "LOLA-Kaguya-Shaded" to see the interactions with the relief. We also suggest that you remove the phase and libration which are of no use in this research by cliking on the appropriate buttons of thr buttons bar.

If one of the selected scientific layers has a legend, it is accessible from the contextual menu associated with the right click of the subscription.

By clicking on the "Whole Globe" button, you will be able to rotate the lunar globe by clicking on it with the left mouse button that you leave pressed.

Click on the "Information" tab. In the "Find" area at the bottom, enter the name of the desired formation. Press the "Find" button.

The formation is then centered on the map in its scientific choosen context (Composition, gravimetry, geological nature ...). Move the "Zoom" slider to magnify the map as desired. Press the "Center formation" button in the button bar to center the formation selected in the "Map" window.




GENERATE DOCUMENTS USABLE IN THE FIELD

If you do not wish to subject your computer to outside weather (cold, wet, wind ...), or if you only have a fixed computer at home, you can print usable documents for your observations, if you have a printer.

In the "Printing" tab of the "Configuration" menu, you can choose the width of the margins of the document to be printed, the size of the characters in the information sheet and the nature of the documents to be printed.

The two most important are the map for finding formations and the information sheet on that formation. So tick at the two corresponding boxes. Note that the two documents can be printed on the same page if you have not chosen a font size that is too large.

In the "Display" tab of the "Configuration" menu, choose the size of the name printing font to make them more readable when printed. You will undoubtedly have to make several tests with different sizes, colors and density of labels to arrive at the compromise which you will prefer.

We also recommend that you choose the colors white or black for marks and labels if you have a black and white printer.

Then choose the magnification factor. At a 1: 1 ratio, the map shows the entire image of the Moon as it is visible with a magnification of approximately 100 times, that is to say almost completely filling the field of a conventional eyepiece.

Then open the "File" menu. Configure your printer using the "Printer selection" choice. Choose the printer from the upper drop-down list, as well as the power mode. We advise you to opt for the "Portrait" format. Then click on the "OK" button to save the choices.

Re-open the "File" menu and choose the "Print" option to send the documents you have chosen to print. You now have a document that can be used "in the field" without the need for a computer on site.

SATELLIZATION AROUND THE MOON

It is possible for you to have an idea of ​​the spectacle that the astronauts of the "Apollo" missions or of the future missions could contemplate from the orbit around the Moon.

Go to the "Display" tab of the "Configuration" menu and uncheck the "Display labels", "Display marks" and "Mark the point of maximum libration" boxes so as not to obstruct the landscape. But you can also leave them checked to recognize the formations overflown. Note that the names are only displayed when the rotation is stopped.

Open the "Textures" tab and choose the ""Virtual Moon Atlas"" texture which is the most "natural" for all zoom levels.

Then just press the "Whole Globe" button in the button bar. In this mode, the operation of the mouse and the elevators of the "Map" window change. The mouse is used to rotate the lunar globe around the axis of the poles and the elevators are used only to center the desired region.

Click on the "Tools" tab on the right and click on the "East" or "West" globe rotation buttons to tilt the lunar globe as desired. The most spectacular is with the equator at the top.

Right click and select your most powerful eyepiece from the context menu that appears. The round cover appears, simulating a "porthole" of a space cabin. Use the elevators of the map window to position yourself at the edge of the globe or use the "East View", "Center" or "West View" buttons in the "Satellization" frame in the "Tools" tab to choose the desired region.

Select the desired orbiting speed from the drop-down list and click on "<" or ">" to choose the direction of movement. The "II" button is used to stop it to allow the display of formation names.

Redo a right click and select from the "Rotation" option to select the speed you want. And now enjoy the show and dream a little ...

STUDY A SPECIFIC CATEGORY OF LUNAR FORMATIONS

With DATLUN (c), the data manager of the "Virtual Moon Atlas", you can select a specific category of formations that you want to locate to study it.

Patrick Chevalley has chosen to base DATLUN (c) on the powerful SQL language so that it can generate very sophisticated multi-criteria queries.

In addition, the information entered into the databases by Christian Legrand is from the start "calibrated" to allow very precise searches.

The combination of these two factors allows you to select, for example, lists of the :

- The rilles over 5 km wide visible 2 days after the First Quarter.
- Craters with a central peak between 20 and 30 km in diameter (Area of ​​appearance of the phenomenon)
- The flat bottom craters filled with dark lava clearly visible in a 50 mm diameter instrument
- The formations of map n ° 33 from "Atlas of the Moon" by Antonin Rükl or from page 158 of Viscardy's Photographic Atlas
- The formations present on the LOPAM IV-108-H2 image around Alphonsus

STUDY THE LUNAR TOPONYMY

With DATLUN (c), the data manager of the "Virtual Moon Atlas", you can also carry out studies on lunar toponymy.

Patrick Chevalley has chosen to base DATLUN (c) on the powerful SQL language so that it can generate very sophisticated multi-criteria queries.

In addition, the information entered into the databases by Christian Legrand is from the start "calibrated" to allow very precise searches.

The combination of these two factors allows you to select, for example, the named formations whose name is taken from characters having the same characteristics, for example:

- Characters who lived in the 18th century
- Characters who are Americanh mathematicians or physicists (or of any other nationality)
- Characters who made studies about light (For example)

STUDY ANCIENT LUNAR MAPS

Start the program. Go to the "Configuration" menu. Select the "Textures" tab. On the "Historical" line of the table, select the historical texture you want to study from the drop-down list. Click on the "OK" button. The historical texture becomes the basic texture. We also suggest that you remove the phase and libration which are of no use in this research using the adequate buttons in the buttons bar.

By clicking on the "Full Globe" button, you will be able to rotate the lunar globe by clicking on it with the left mouse button that you leave pressed. Of course, the Farside of the Moon is not mapped.

Click on the "Information" tab. Enter the name of the desired formation in the "Find" area. Press the "Find" button.

The formation is then centered on the map on the historical map. Move the "Zoom" slider to magnify the map as desired. Press the "Center" button to center the chosen formation in the "Map" window. It is likely that the label of the formation is not located on it given the uncertainties of the drawings made at these times.

By pressing the "2nd Window" button, you can bring up a new window and adjust the display with another texture as before, allowing you to conduct particularly enriching comparative studies.

The active window is surrounded by a red border. Just click on one of the two windows to make it active.
